## Summary

Mike and Matthias unpack a 10-day autonomous agent run that produced an orchestration platform through daily steering rather than constant hands-on coding. They share concrete output stats (hundreds of PRs, dozens of releases, and high test volume) and discuss what made the loop reliable: delegation, self-review, and rapid issue/backlog iteration. The conversation emphasizes a multi-harness strategy to avoid vendor lock-in and use different agents/models for implementation, review, and orchestration. They also showcase the new agenticthinking.show site built with agents, including command-palette style navigation and transcript search. The episode closes with why open semantic standards like OSI could improve enterprise AI context portability.

## Key Takeaways

- ▸ A long-running “manager” agent can deliver substantial output with light daily steering when it is explicitly instructed to delegate implementation to worker agents.
- ▸ The showcased run reported extreme throughput (e.g., hundreds of PRs/releases) while staying in one extended context window, highlighting what sustained agent loops can do.
- ▸ Combining harnesses by role (for example, one for orchestration, another for coding, another for review) was presented as a practical way to reduce lock-in and improve results.
- ▸ Reliability improved because new releases were immediately used for subsequent work, causing regressions to surface quickly and be fed back into the backlog.
- ▸ The hosts call out a shift from mutable low-level tests to higher-level behavior contracts so agents cannot silently “fix” tests by changing expected behavior.
- ▸ They also demoed an agent-built podcast site with Control+K commands, episode search, and transcript-linked navigation as a real production example of agentic delivery.
